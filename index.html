Different kinds of data and related metadata

There are many different kinds of data! Below are some examples of different kinds of data we might find our example from Editions de l'Oiseau-Lyre Archive. Music for performance and publication.

  • Qualitative (text) data - like letters, clippings, interviews, and diaries. In our specific example, the descriptions found in the "Notes" metadata field of the record. (We look at ways of visualising based data in more detail below.)
  • Quantitative (numbers) data - like counts of scores, reaction times, number of publications per composer over time. In our specific example, the number of manuscripts per item as found in the "Physical description" metadata field. (We look at ways of visualising numbers data in more detail below.)
  • Locations - like city, country, and coordinates. In our specific example, it could be the birth place of the composer found in the "Author" metadata field. (We look at ways of visulising location based data in more detail below.)
  • Time series - like publications over time. In our specific example, it could be the year in which the piece was published found in the "Published" metadata field. (We do not look at this kind of data in this workshop, but see the next steps section for suggestions if you're interested!)
  • The music itself! - like scores, scores represented as xml, and recordings. In our specific example, it could be the actual item described in the "Physical description" metadata field (We do not look at this kind of data in this workshop, but see the next steps section for suggestions if you're interested!)

2.3.1 Sorting out the metadata

There are lots of things to keep in mind when you're working with metadata, we're just going to touch on a few now. You may hear of Metadata Standards when talking about metadata. These are a pre defined set of metadata fields and ways of expressing data and metadata so that it is consistent in your own datasets and across different peoples datasets. Example: Dublin Core. Pros include that it is easy to quickly understand lots of different datasets, everything is displayed in a consistent way. Cons are that you have to get everyone working in a field to agree to do things the same way!

It is great if you can use a standard in your own data, but the most important thing is to be consistent. That is, use the same metadata fields in your own data, structure your data and metadata in a consistent way, and describe and document everythign you do.

The "richer" your metadata, the more you can do with your dataset. For example, if you have nice, clear, detailed metadata fields, you can do lots of interesting visualisations and perform really good searches. We'll look more at this in section 3 below.

2.3.2 Cleaning up the data

"Data Cleaning" can mean many things. Today, we're going to think of "data cleaning" from the perspective of having to separate out data into different metadata fields - the fields we just added above. But, data cleaning can also refer to standardising data, to formatting data in a way that is suitable for analysis, and so on.

Note: There are interesting discussions around the ethics of data cleaning - pragmatically it is needed so that we can make sense of the data, but it also means removing and "smoothing over" certain elements of a dataset. If this is of interest, we can do a further workshop on this at a later date.

2.3.3 Establishing your data management plan

A data management plan is a document in which you describe your data, and how you plan to store and preserve it. The University of Melbourne has a course on data management called Managing Data @Melbourne. This is a good resource to get you started. It is written for a general audience, so if you're interested in a more tailored plan for your work, or if this is of interest, we can do a further workshop on this at a later date.

    3.1 Methods for text data

    A really great tool for text based analysis is Voyant. Our data isn't really text heavy, but we can still use Voyant to explore some of the fields. Let's hop over to Voyant now for to explore. We're going to work just with the Notes field in our example dataset. Functionalities we will look at include:

    • Wordcloud, including stop words
    • TermsBerry, including Top terms and Distinct terms
    • Contexts, including sentence and paragraph level

    There are many other things you can do with Voyant, and it's really easy and fun to explore! It can be really powerful for longer texts like letters, or program notes. Note: if there are ethical considerations around your data (e.g. if you needed UoM ethics to collect it, if you need to be careful who has access to it) then do not use Voyant. We can discuss alternatives if this is something you need to consider.

    3.2 Methods for numbers data

    For visualising quantitative data, we're going to use a tool you all know: Excel. Like Voyant, Excel is just one tool of many to use for data visualisation. You can do everything and more using a coding language (Python, R), but you can also do a lot with Excel and it is a great way to start exploring the possibilities without having to learn everything at once. We're going to work just with the Document Type field that we created above. Let’s hop over to excel now and take a look. Functionalities we'll look at include:

    • Sort and filter, really good for counting items in groups
    • Pivot tables, really good for looking at how items group together
    • Graphs, really good for making visualisations directly in Excel

    If you're interested in mapping, or if you have location-based data, you can create some really nice maps using freely available software. Let's think about this from the perspective of plotting composer birthplaces from the "Author" field. Functionalities we'll discuss include

    The main way that people share their data is using a repository (a digital archive of data and code, but also NTROs and other outputs).     4.2 Showcasing your data and visualisations

    Figshare is a repository - it's designed to data and visualisations in the long term. But it's not always the best for "showcasing" these. That is, displaying the data nicely for something like a digital exhibition. You might want to do this if you have lots of nice visualisations from your work, or if you're part of a project where others are creating visualisations, or if you want to make it nice and easy for people to see items that you think are important. There are lots of tools you can use to do this, but we're going to look today at Omeka. Unlike Figshare, you can't just log into a UoM Omeka account - you have to request one from RCS. However, today we'll look at an Omeka site that I've been part of in the past, and talk through the functionalities. We'll look at:

    • The "website" part of Omeka: unlike Fighsare, where it's a collection of items, Omeka looks more like a website.
    • Items
    • Collections

    4.3 What are the benifits?

    There are many benefits to sharing your data and visualisations, here are just a few:

    • Persistent identifiers for your research outputs, making them citable.
    • Your research outputs are richly described, making it easier for future you to remember what you did years later
    • Building a portfolio of research activity that is open access

    Even if you do not end up sharing research outputs, it is good practice to approach the work like you will because it encourages you to document your steps - this will ultimately help you in the long run when you have to make sense of what you did in the future!
